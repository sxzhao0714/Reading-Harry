# Chapter 1 — The Boy Who Lived

Mr. and Mrs. Dursley, of number four, Privet Drive, were proud to say that they were perfectly normal, thank you very much. They were the last people you’d expect to be involved in anything strange or <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="cd5736a0-7756-4a82-93c3-963c7b8cea79">mysterious</mark>, because they just <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="61cc4e67-62cf-4771-a655-af2e90789123">didn’t hold with</mark> such nonsense.

Mr. Dursley was the <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="502eedb5-5a19-4244-907e-e457ed3e3826">director of a firm</mark> called Grunnings, which made <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="5bc2c990-5f23-4a45-9fb7-0950f84a4772">drills</mark>. He was a big, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="465f77bc-e7bb-409d-a9ac-146995d2e314">beefy</mark> man with hardly any neck, although he did have a very large <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="5abf76bf-122a-4710-8d03-13ec5ff4e911">mustache</mark>. Mrs. Dursley was thin and blonde and had nearly twice the usual amount of neck, which came in very useful as she spent so much of her time <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="4c42e459-9241-4bca-97b9-f57001fed181">craning</mark> over garden <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="502084a0-3373-45ae-aec5-2421733beee0">fences</mark>, spying on the neighbors. The Dursleys had a small son called Dudley and in their opinion there was no finer boy anywhere.

The Dursleys had everything they wanted, but they also had a secret, and their greatest fear was that somebody would discover it. They didn’t think they could bear it if anyone found out about the Potters. Mrs. Potter was Mrs. Dursley’s sister, but they hadn’t met for several years; in fact, Mrs. Dursley pretended she didn’t have a sister, because her sister and her good-for-nothing husband were as unDursleyish as it was possible to be. The Dursleys <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="dfaf2c5e-dbfe-4b13-8b8a-7bdb13f3c9d4">shuddered</mark> to think what the neighbors would say if the Potters arrived in the street. The Dursleys knew that the Potters had a small son, too, but they had never even seen him. This boy was another good reason for keeping the Potters away; they didn’t want Dudley mixing with a child like that.

When Mr. and Mrs. Dursley woke up <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="86aa011e-499d-4c92-8a33-d3cde9720122">on the dull</mark>, gray Tuesday our story starts, there was nothing about the cloudy sky outside to suggest that strange and mysterious things would soon be happening all over the country. Mr. Dursley <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="c05dc085-362c-42b8-866e-dd789f193395">hummed</mark> as he picked out his most boring tie for work, and Mrs. Dursley gossiped away happily as she <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="11eb5a46-972b-4c8c-a5fb-267d88bd4e15">wrestled</mark> a screaming Dudley into his high chair.

None of them noticed a large, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="5d565f4a-8443-4fc3-99d4-4f7a6ff4a76f">tawny</mark> owl flutter past the window.

At half past eight, Mr. Dursley picked up his <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="31160691-fc0b-450e-ad14-996f6f0b87f2">briefcase</mark>, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="9231d5fb-6e36-4632-b91c-d10b1b263609">pecked</mark> Mrs. Dursley on the <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="d8a751f5-3b54-49e7-8f55-b242dec0dad5">cheek</mark>, and tried to kiss Dudley good-bye but missed, because Dudley was now having a <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="43860c4d-81e1-40b4-b84b-ea2cde3b2ad1">tantrum</mark> and throwing his <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="b0777f48-ad66-4171-901c-358476767e93">cereal</mark> at the walls. “Little <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="1aa6ac2c-b6e1-41c7-81ef-773d87ae3d1f">tyke</mark>,” <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="d31b6592-8bdd-48d3-9148-faec4056646c">chortled</mark> Mr. Dursley as he left the house. He got into his car and backed out of number four’s drive.

It was on the corner of the street that he noticed the first sign of something <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="b430ab62-c1f2-4fde-bd7d-0a665d6688e5">peculiar</mark> — a cat reading a map. For a second, Mr. Dursley didn’t realize what he had seen — then he <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-blue" data-id="d5e90b78-0bfb-47a1-af4d-7cc1f3c1d7d4">jerked his head around</mark> to look again. There was a <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="23a7a186-1267-4510-b270-5cb5ce39c284">tabby</mark> cat standing on the corner of Privet Drive, but there wasn’t a map in sight. What could he have been thinking of? It must have been <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="ffda1d51-0d26-443c-963d-325ba1ac50e9">a trick of the light</mark>. Mr. Dursley blinked and stared at the cat. It stared back. As Mr. Dursley drove around the corner and up the road, he watched the cat in his mirror. It was now reading the sign that said Privet Drive — no, looking at the sign; cats couldn’t read maps or signs. Mr. Dursley gave himself a little shake and put the cat out of his mind. As he drove toward town he thought of nothing except a large order of drills he was hoping to get that day.

But on the <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="44aab055-9a07-46ea-a1af-32c475094278">edge</mark> of town, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="3ae9aaac-2962-41ae-9ba3-98a4944d8cd9">drills were driven out of his mind by something else</mark>. As he sat in the usual morning traffic jam, he couldn’t help noticing that there seemed to be a lot of strangely dressed people about. People in <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="782f9a55-1c4d-4d8b-a6b2-ca31f6879a96">cloaks</mark>. Mr. Dursley couldn’t bear people who dressed in funny clothes — the <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="a825514e-42b3-4004-be94-c84b3a5587bf">getups</mark> you saw on young people! He supposed this was some stupid new fashion. He <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="a453227a-6082-477d-8d93-77cfb5b14da4">drummed his fingers</mark> on the <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="c8549a66-d43b-476f-b201-3cbca6e06c62">steering wheel</mark> and <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="eddd1b94-9c48-4a0d-8f27-380ad308fecb">his eyes fell on a huddle of these weirdos standing quite close by</mark>. They were <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="451c0962-c288-48a4-acc8-a87802f970f3">whispering</mark> excitedly together. Mr. Dursley was <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="ff47daaf-87c4-4061-9188-fde82486fcc4">enraged</mark> to see that a couple of them weren’t young at all; why, that man had to be older than he was, and wearing an <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="e515cba6-4ad6-4c06-9424-40b930c52560">emerald-green</mark> cloak! <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-blue" data-id="13cdcd7d-33f8-492e-a303-1251a4f7394c">The nerve of him!</mark> <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="81e8c43c-a0df-4824-83f9-1120e099c44e">But then it struck Mr. Dursley that this was probably some silly stunt</mark> — these people were obviously <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="bd82f2e1-265f-4d5d-9cae-c4a5ca6b4ad6">collecting</mark> for something … yes, that would be it. The traffic moved on and a few minutes later, Mr. Dursley arrived in the Grunnings <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="bf922247-29f6-48ea-8d98-37d2cca1d3bd">parking lot</mark>, his mind back on drills.

Mr. Dursley always sat with his back to the window in his office on the ninth floor. If he hadn’t, he might have found it harder to concentrate on drills that morning. <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="36cd985b-cf64-4c5a-8e86-e0508d4122f4">He didn’t see the owls swooping past in broad daylight</mark>, though people <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="c8558e0e-b748-4550-a5c3-b80762a37a7f">down in the street</mark> did; they pointed and <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="464b2f4d-7677-4419-be2c-6bf5ed2ed336">gazed</mark> <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="af6dfa06-0bba-4ed4-b88f-dc08c7869607">open-mouthed</mark> as owl after owl <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="a9b09b4f-d95c-4327-bb2a-9418a876437e">sped</mark> overhead. Most of them had never seen an owl even at nighttime. Mr. Dursley, however, had a perfectly normal, owl-free morning. He yelled at five different people. He made several important telephone calls and shouted a bit more. He was in a very good mood until lunchtime, when he thought he’d <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="7e308fa0-1636-4a8a-a292-b4e4ed82c96b">stretch</mark> his legs and walk across the road to buy himself a <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="ecf12f0d-6a4d-4d70-b6ee-f03cd2b9ff6a">bun</mark> from the <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="c9467de9-f942-4cb4-a55d-2178b19efbdb">bakery</mark>.

He’d forgotten all about the people in cloaks until he passed a group of them next to the baker’s. He eyed them angrily as he passed. He didn’t know why, but they made him <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="cc33e29f-c4cc-4381-b8ea-c3a5fc2665fc">uneasy</mark>. This <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="ea16f617-f348-41b7-8ac5-c0d96587f1de">bunch</mark> were whispering excitedly, too, and he couldn’t see a single <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="19af2575-c924-41d7-bc4a-b0dc215f2cef">collecting tin</mark>. It was on his way back past them, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="1be18c2e-fbae-488a-8b32-14a50049e0b0">clutching</mark> a large <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="51d1a2b4-05b8-4cc0-a653-2df829d497ca">doughnut</mark> in a bag, that he caught a few words of what they were saying.

“The Potters, that’s right, that’s what I heard —”

“— yes, their son, Harry —”

<mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-blue" data-id="d3a19290-dc86-47fd-9412-d02436643b9c">Mr. Dursley stopped dead.</mark>Fear <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="f9e994de-89f3-4ce1-b5ab-c8014bc79f64">flooded</mark> him. He looked back at the whisperers as if he wanted to say something to them, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="c5d04adf-7009-4553-a27f-29f4dc201e7d">but thought better of it</mark>.

He <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="2778ecfc-0ec5-4f1b-b7ca-45a039e19251">dashed</mark> back across the road, hurried up to his office, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="03423132-5074-4286-844c-fa80487c69e9">snapped</mark> at his secretary not to disturb him, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="b408bc20-0e34-4c7e-8327-dbbc69cf3196">seized</mark> his telephone, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="b9cf3170-8db5-4e3d-84ad-0b8103092da6">and had almost finished dialing his home number when he changed his mind.</mark> He put the <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="13ce9d7e-7b8e-4307-92ea-bc62277c4a96">receiver</mark> back down and <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="2c970e11-d788-4a26-8f5b-4d32724fc56c">stroked</mark> his <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="5815f05e-6768-49b7-b2e9-f24cff7de4ad">mustache</mark>, thinking … no, he was being stupid. Potter wasn’t such an unusual name. He was sure there were lots of people called Potter who had a son called Harry. Come to think of it, he wasn’t even sure his nephew was called Harry. He’d never even seen the boy. It might have been Harvey. Or Harold. <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="2a71a48f-f464-4878-94e3-56fbeb471895">There was no point in worrying Mrs. Dursley</mark>; she always got so upset at any mention of her sister. He didn’t blame her — if he’d had a sister like that … <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="324c97a8-21f3-42a8-945e-dec260f9b52a">but all the same</mark>, those people in cloaks …

He found it a lot harder to concentrate on drills that afternoon and when he left the building at five o’clock, he was still so worried that he walked straight into someone just outside the door.

“Sorry,” he <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="fb33e02b-5c04-4611-a7ed-a760a9333c2b">grunted</mark>, as the tiny old man <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="41a202f5-3149-4f1a-a920-90af2052dbf1">stumbled</mark> and almost fell. It was a few seconds before Mr. Dursley realized that the man was wearing a <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="7815fc01-d8f1-46fb-aef8-d17ac6a2d38a">violet</mark> cloak. <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="8e78c1c0-e14d-46a7-aa6c-4eacf0a15743">He didn’t seem at all upset at being almost knocked to the ground.</mark> On the contrary, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-blue" data-id="9efd4330-61cb-4366-95dc-42d6c5cad4df">his face split into a wide smile</mark> and he said in a <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="ab80547d-5152-4691-815e-d72abb28db3d">squeaky</mark> voice that made <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="8c9a2654-3c3b-450b-b5d0-ca8ea3b1cd55">passersby</mark> stare, “Don’t be sorry, my dear sir, for nothing could upset me today! <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="8bbff230-50eb-40e8-938c-8eb06192187f">Rejoice</mark>, for You-Know-Who has gone <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="19c997cd-aa33-4529-9a9e-b746c6e231b5">at last</mark>! Even Muggles like yourself should be celebrating, this happy, happy day!”

And the old man <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="7605b2fc-b865-4543-b637-d909a0076686">hugged Mr. Dursley around the middle</mark> and walked off.

<mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-blue" data-id="7be8b5de-29f3-4a88-8104-cb89e08075f6">Mr. Dursley stood rooted to the spot.</mark> He had been hugged by a complete stranger. He also thought he had been called a Muggle, whatever that was. He was <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="3f411bef-f518-4f45-b49a-16658def621d">rattled</mark>. He hurried to his car and set off for home, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="42225447-c570-4f5c-83e5-6a354654fc2e">hoping he was imagining things, which he had never hoped before</mark>, because he didn’t <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="3095316d-701a-4b5a-af2a-6015f80152dc">approve</mark> of imagination.

As he pulled into the driveway of number four, the first thing he saw — and it didn’t improve his mood — was the tabby cat he’d <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="d945a7fa-f023-4561-8dbe-1ed60e063d68">spotted</mark> that morning. It was now sitting on his garden wall. He was sure it was the same one; it had the same markings around its eyes.

“Shoo!” said Mr. Dursley loudly.

The cat didn’t move. It just gave him a <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="7bb957bd-1605-4fc9-b016-51888d8cb34b">stern</mark> look. Was this normal cat behavior? Mr. Dursley wondered. Trying to pull himself together, he let himself into the house. He was still determined not to mention anything to his wife.

Mrs. Dursley had had a nice, normal day. She told him over dinner all about Mrs. Next Door’s problems with her daughter and how Dudley had learned a new word (“Won’t!”). Mr. Dursley tried to act normally. When Dudley had been put to bed, he went into the living room <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="6e5b0aa3-dbaf-464a-bf38-aa4a561276d8">in time to catch</mark> the last report on the evening news:

“And finally, bird-watchers everywhere have reported that the nation’s owls have been behaving very unusually today. Although owls normally <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="48d19488-87ed-42d8-9d86-2bd0076dc439">hunt</mark> at night and are hardly ever seen in daylight, there have been hundreds of <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="4431d172-d773-4dcd-b9c7-6ef969cc7654">sightings</mark> of these birds flying in every <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="684b8650-d824-4d21-8f32-d8598e265a33">direction</mark> since sunrise. Experts are unable to explain why the owls have suddenly changed their sleeping <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="d905e175-3d93-4b30-a2d6-0af2c2692ef5">pattern</mark>.” The <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="0a890871-0c52-4423-934c-eb80a3313f2d">newscaster</mark> allowed himself a <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="af62d567-5e66-438d-a1c2-4fa9c2f25cba">grin</mark>. “Most mysterious. And now, over to Jim McGuffin with the weather. <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-blue" data-id="dcc2605c-e6fc-478c-8b1e-968f6c93636c">Going to be any more showers of owls tonight</mark>, Jim?”

“Well, Ted,” said the weatherman, “I don’t know about that, but it’s not only the owls that have been acting <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="ed50d331-e376-403a-a0f9-38755280ec08">oddly</mark> today. <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="e6414f25-1933-48e5-8b51-b699a1d89272">Viewers</mark> as far apart as Kent, Yorkshire, and Dundee have been phoning in to tell me that instead of the rain I promised yesterday, they’ve had a <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="641a8eaa-8dca-46e8-8659-de9c726e9a23">downpour</mark> of <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="b0b5093c-15dd-4e47-a438-c589f93befa7">shooting stars</mark>! Perhaps people have been celebrating Bonfire Night early — it’s not until next week, folks! But I can promise a wet night tonight.”

Mr. Dursley sat <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="8e927ba8-d731-4fe8-adfe-5ab1440b128e">frozen</mark> in his armchair. Shooting stars all over Britain? Owls flying by daylight? Mysterious people in cloaks all over the place? And a whisper, a whisper about the Potters …

Mrs. Dursley came into the living room carrying two cups of tea. It was no good. He’d have to say something to her. <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-blue" data-id="63e9865f-9d0c-43db-842b-22acdb8091ac">He cleared his throat nervously</mark>. “Er — Petunia, dear — you haven’t heard from your sister lately, have you?”

As he had expected, Mrs. Dursley looked shocked and angry. After all, they normally pretended she didn’t have a sister.

“No,” she said sharply. “Why?”

“Funny stuff on the news,” Mr. Dursley <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="2688c0da-a1c2-4bd8-b1bc-abd3a3e3c94b">mumbled</mark>. “Owls … shooting stars … and there were a lot of funny-looking people in town today …”

“So?” <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="86ef7018-ec04-4e2a-9723-32a1bd937c99">snapped</mark> Mrs. Dursley.

“Well, I just thought … maybe … it was something to do with … you know … her <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="0f60cd5b-42d3-4528-9b29-08162ae9dae5">crowd</mark>.”

Mrs. Dursley <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="e1a951c9-a3af-4d38-bc47-42d9d4e81954">sipped</mark> her tea through <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="9973ef8d-76a4-42ee-ba78-41bad18240db">pursed</mark> lips. Mr. Dursley wondered whether he dared tell her he’d heard the name “Potter.” He decided he didn’t dare. Instead he said, as casually as he could, “Their son — he’d be about Dudley’s age now, wouldn’t he?”

“I suppose so,” said Mrs. Dursley <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="ee287161-c90d-4e34-8898-42a195628ae0">stiffly</mark>.

“What’s his name again? Howard, isn’t it?”

“Harry. <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="3ee01b6c-fe9d-4562-90b7-8f81330a123a">Nasty</mark>, common name, if you ask me.”

“Oh, yes,” said Mr. Dursley, his heart <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="ebfb2909-4128-4b4c-b3e2-49697e8178df">sinking</mark> horribly. “Yes, I quite agree.”

He didn’t say another word on the subject as they went upstairs to bed. While Mrs. Dursley was in the bathroom, Mr. Dursley <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="a3e05429-0bf1-4e4f-b975-5ea5b11e8f20">crept</mark> to the bedroom window and <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="1e171a6b-50f4-436f-96a5-582b5e53c752">peered</mark> down into the front garden. The cat was still there. It was staring down Privet Drive as though it were waiting for something.

Was he imagining things? Could all this have anything to do with the Potters? If it did … <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-green" data-id="69c53df1-99f7-43b8-9342-877ce8eaffe0">if it got out that they were related to a pair of</mark> — well, he didn’t think he could bear it.

The Dursleys got into bed. Mrs. Dursley fell asleep quickly but Mr. Dursley lay awake, turning it all over in his mind. His last, <mark class="eme-highlight eme-h-yellow" data-id="a385250e-9959-4bad-957f-86006b88e290">comforting</mark> thought before he fell asleep was that even if the Potters were involved, there was no reason for them to come near him and Mrs. Dursley. ### 精彩表达（Blue）

- **jerked his head around**
  - 含义：猛地转过头去
  - 用法：jerk one's head around，形容突然、急促地转头看
  - 模仿：She jerked her head around when she heard her name called.
- **The nerve of him!**
  - 含义：他真放肆！/ 他真不要脸！
  - 用法：The nerve of sb! 表示对某人厚颜无耻行为的愤慨，口语感叹句
  - 模仿：The nerve of him, asking for a raise after being late every day!
- **Mr. Dursley stopped dead.**
  - 含义：德思礼先生猛地停住了脚步
  - 用法：stop dead 表示突然完全停下，dead 作副词强调"完全、彻底"
  - 模仿：When I saw the bill, I stopped dead in my tracks.
- **his face split into a wide smile**
  - 含义：他的脸上绽开了一个大大的笑容
  - 用法：split into a smile 形容笑容突然绽放，split 强调笑得灿烂
  - 模仿：Her face split into a wide smile when she saw the puppy.
- **Mr. Dursley stood rooted to the spot.**
  - 含义：德思礼先生像生了根一样站在原地
  - 用法：stand rooted to the spot/ground，比喻因震惊或恐惧而呆立不动
  - 模仿：When the alarm went off, I stood rooted to the spot for a second.
- **Going to be any more showers of owls tonight**
  - 含义：今晚还会有更多的猫头鹰雨吗？
  - 用法：showers of... 形容大量某物如雨般落下，此处为新闻主播的幽默双关
  - 模仿：Are there going to be any more showers of complaints this week?
- **He cleared his throat nervously.**
  - 含义：他紧张地清了清嗓子
  - 用法：clear one's throat 清嗓子，常用于开口说话前的犹豫或紧张
  - 模仿：He cleared his throat nervously before making the announcement.
- **its eyes narrowed**
  - 含义：它的眼睛眯了起来
  - 用法：narrow one's eyes 眯起眼睛，表示怀疑、审视或不满
  - 模仿：The cat's eyes narrowed as it watched the stranger approach.
- **jerked her head**
  - 含义：猛地一甩头
  - 用法：jerk one's head 猛然转头或甩头，表示方向或强调
  - 模仿：She jerked her head toward the door, signaling him to leave.
- **how in the name of heaven**
  - 含义：天哪，到底怎么……
  - 用法：in the name of heaven/heaven's name，强调语气的疑问，相当于"到底"
  - 模仿：How in the name of heaven did you manage to finish so quickly?
- **jumping to her feet**
  - 含义：猛地站起来
  - 用法：jump/spring to one's feet，形容迅速站起，常因惊讶或愤怒
  - 模仿：She jumped to her feet when she heard the verdict.

### 句型 / 语法（Green）

- **didn't hold with**（短语动词）
  - 含义：不赞同；不容忍
  - 结构 / 用法：not hold with sth/doing sth，表示不赞成某事
  - 例：She doesn't hold with staying up late on school nights.
- **on the dull**（句型）
  - 含义：在那个阴沉的……
  - 结构 / 用法：on the + adj.，用于强调某天的特征，文学性表达
  - 例：On the cold, gray morning, nothing seemed to go right.
- **a trick of the light**（习语）
  - 含义：光线造成的错觉
  - 结构 / 用法：a trick of the light，固定搭配，形容因光线导致的视觉误差
  - 例：I thought I saw someone at the window, but it must have been a trick of the light.
- **drills were driven out of his mind by something else**（句型）
  - 含义：钻头的事被别的事情从脑海中赶了出去
  - 结构 / 用法：被动语态 + be driven out of one's mind，表示某事被完全取代/遗忘
  - 例：All my worries were driven out of my mind by the good news.
- **drummed his fingers**（短语动词）
  - 含义：用手指敲击（表示不耐烦）
  - 结构 / 用法：drum one's fingers on sth，表示不耐烦地用手指敲打

- **his eyes fell on a huddle of these weirdos standing quite close by**（句型）

  - 例：She had almost finished the report when the computer crashed.
